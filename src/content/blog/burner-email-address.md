A free trial wants your email before it will show you anything. So do the e-book, the discount code, and the forum you plan to visit exactly once. You would rather not hand your real address to every one of them and spend the next year deleting what they send.

> A burner email address is a temporary, throwaway address you use once and then abandon. You point it at a signup, free trial, or gated download you don't want tied to your real inbox. Services like 10 Minute Mail and Guerrilla Mail generate one with no sign-up, and the inbox self-destructs on a timer. It is disposable by design.

## What is a burner email address?

A burner email address is a disposable inbox you create for a single interaction and never touch again. The name borrows from burner phones: cheap, anonymous, thrown away once they have done their one job. You use it to receive one confirmation link or one download, then you walk away and let it expire.

The point is separation. Whatever you sign up for only ever learns the burner, so your real address stays out of that company's database, its marketing exports, and any breach that leaks its list later. Your address is a tracking key that follows you across sites, and a valuable one. We put a [dollar figure on what a subscriber is worth](/blog/what-is-your-email-address-worth) in a separate piece; the short version is that keeping your real address out of circulation pays off.

## Burner, disposable, throwaway, temp mail: same thing?

Mostly, yes. Burner, disposable, throwaway, and temporary email all describe the same idea: an address meant to be used once and discarded. People swap the words freely, and for everyday purposes they mean the same thing.

One distinction is worth knowing. A temp-mail service hands you an anonymous, receive-only inbox with no sign-up and an automatic delete timer. A burner in the stricter sense is a real account on Gmail or Outlook that you open with minimal details and use for a while before abandoning it. The temp-mail kind is faster and leaves nothing behind. The real-account kind lasts longer and gets past sites that block throwaway domains. Most of this article covers the temp-mail kind, since that is what people reach for first.

## How does a temp-mail service actually work?

A temp-mail service runs mail servers that accept mail for any address under their domains, then shows you the inbox in your browser. You open the page, it assigns you an address like `a7f2k@guerrillamail.com`, and any mail sent there appears within seconds. No password, no account, no personal details.

Two things define the experience. First, the inbox is disposable. It self-destructs on a timer that runs from minutes to a few days depending on the service. 10 Minute Mail gives you ten minutes with a button to extend it. [Guerrilla Mail](https://www.guerrillamail.com/) keeps the address alive for about an hour and even lets you compose a reply. Second, and this is the catch, most temp inboxes are public. There is no password, so anyone who knows or guesses the full address can read what lands in it. Guerrilla Mail offers a scrambled-address option to make guessing harder, which tells you the plain default is not private.

## When does a burner email make sense?

A burner earns its place any time you need to receive one thing and never hear from that sender again. The test is simple: will you ever need to log back in, reset a password, or recover this account? If the honest answer is no, a burner fits.

Good candidates:

- **One-off signups.** A forum, a wiki, or a tool you are trying once and will not return to.
- **Free trials and gated content.** The e-book, whitepaper, webinar, or discount code that demands an email before it hands over the goods.
- **Contest and giveaway entries.** These exist largely to build marketing lists. A burner takes the entry, and the follow-up flood lands nowhere.
- **Sketchy or one-time sites.** A download mirror, a store you have never heard of, a site whose privacy practices you do not trust.
- **Testing your own signup forms.** Developers use them to check registration and reset flows without cluttering a real inbox.

A burner also quietly absorbs the marketing that follows. The promos and the [open tracking and link redirects](/blog/email-tracking-beyond-the-pixel) a sender staples to every message fire into an inbox nobody reads. You take the one message you came for and let the address die.

## When should you not use a burner email?

Never use a burner for anything you might need again. This is the mistake that catches people out. A burner is built to disappear, so the moment a site needs to reach you a second time, the address is already gone.

Three limits bite in practice. First, account recovery breaks completely. If you register a real account with a temp address and later need a password reset, the reset email goes to an inbox you no longer control, and you are locked out with no way back in. Second, the public-inbox problem. On most temp services anyone who knows the address can read it, so never route anything sensitive through a burner, whether that is a verification code, a receipt, or a document you care about. Third, a burner does nothing for the mail you already get. It protects the next signup and cannot touch the two hundred senders already mailing your real address.

So skip the burner for your bank, your primary shopping accounts, work tools, anything with a payment method attached, and any login you expect to keep.

## Why do some websites reject burner emails?

Because they keep public lists of known throwaway domains and refuse to accept them. Trial abuse and fake signups cost companies money, so many validate the domain part of your address against a blocklist the moment you submit the form.

These lists are large and openly maintained. The community-run [disposable-email-domains](https://github.com/disposable-email-domains/disposable-email-domains) project has curated known temp-mail domains since 2014, and aggregated blocklists such as [eramitgupta/disposable-email](https://github.com/eramitgupta/disposable-email) track more than 110,000 domains and refresh daily (2026). When a signup rejects your address as invalid, a blocklist is usually the reason. The busiest services get blacklisted fastest, which is why some temp providers rotate their free domains constantly to stay one step ahead.

## Burner vs alias vs plus-addressing: which should you use?

They sit on a spectrum from casual to durable, and the right pick depends on whether you ever want the mail to reach you again.

| | Plus-addressing | Masked alias | Burner / temp mail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Setup | Free, instant, built in | Free or cheap, one service | Free, instant, no account |
| Where mail lands | Your real inbox | Your real inbox, forwarded | A throwaway inbox you abandon |
| Reveals your real address? | Yes, tag is easy to strip | No | No |
| Survives password reset | Yes | Yes | No |
| Reduces the mail you get | No | No, until you kill the alias | Yes, it never reaches you |
| Often blocked by sites | Rarely | Rarely | Frequently |
| Best for | Sorting and filtering | Signups you may keep | One-shot, throwaway use |

Bottom line: reach for plus-addressing when you just want to label where mail came from, [which Gmail and Outlook both do natively](/blog/plus-addressing-gmail). Reach for a [masked forwarding alias](/blog/masked-email-aliases-explained) when you want the mail but not your real address exposed, and you might use the account again. Reach for a burner only when you are certain you will never need that inbox after today.
