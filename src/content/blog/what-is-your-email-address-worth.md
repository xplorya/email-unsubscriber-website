You handed a store your email address for ten percent off, and now brands you have never heard of mail you every morning. Somewhere between that checkout page and your inbox, your address turned into an asset that companies buy, sell, and work hard to keep.

> Your email address is worth two very different numbers. In bulk on breach markets it fetches a fraction of a cent, about half a million addresses per dollar in Privacy Affairs' 2023 index. To a marketer who gets you to open and buy, it can return $36 or more per $1 (Litmus, 2025). A confirmed, active address earns the premium.

## How much is your email address worth?

It depends entirely on who holds it and whether they can prove you are real. A single address sitting in a leaked database is worth almost nothing on its own, because the same file holds tens of millions more just like it. The value appears the moment someone confirms a live person reads that address and can be sold something. That confirmation is the whole game, and the rest of this post follows the money through it.

## What is a subscriber worth to a marketer?

Enough to be their most profitable channel. Litmus surveyed nearly 500 marketing professionals for its [2025 State of Email report](https://www.litmus.com/blog/infographic-the-roi-of-email-marketing) and found the largest group, 30 percent, earning between $36 and $50 back for every $1 spent on email, with another 35 percent earning $10 to $36. The UK Data & Marketing Association put the figure at roughly [£42 returned per £1](https://www.emailmonday.com/email-marketing-roi-statistics/) in its 2019 Marketer Email Tracker. No paid channel comes close.

Email pays this well because the sender owns the connection. There is no ad auction, no platform taking a cut, no algorithm deciding who sees the message. A brand that has your address can reach you directly, again and again, for years, at almost no marginal cost. Each subscriber also carries a lifetime value: a shopper who spends even a few dollars a month adds up over a multi-year relationship. That math is why losing you stings, and why the opt-out is buried.

## What does your address sell for on breach and data-broker markets?

In bulk, almost nothing. Privacy Affairs, a privacy research group, compiled its [2023 Dark Web Price Index](https://www.privacyaffairs.com/dark-web-price-index-2023/) from listings across dark-web marketplaces during 2022 and early 2023. It found 100 million U.S. email addresses selling for about $200, and 10 million for about $120. That works out to roughly half a million addresses for a single dollar. A hacked Gmail account, with the inbox attached, went for about $60, because access to a live mailbox is a different product from a raw address in a spreadsheet.

The aboveground market prices the same commodity higher, once it is cleaned. Consumer email lists rented through legitimate brokers commonly run [$100 to $400 per thousand names](https://www.bookyourdata.com/blog/how-much-are-email-lists-worth) according to 2026 industry pricing guides, and opt-in lists cost more than scraped ones. The gap between a fraction of a cent and forty cents an address is the gap between a raw dump and a verified, permission-based list that will actually reach a human.

## Why is a "confirmed live" address worth more?

Because it reaches a real inbox and gets opened. Budget lists scraped from the web often deliver to only half or two-thirds of their addresses, and the rest bounce and damage the sender's reputation. A confirmed-live address skips that waste. Better still is a confirmed-active one, an address whose owner opens messages and clicks, because that person converts. A verified, engaged address sits at the top of the price sheet, and a random scraped one sits at the bottom.

Confirmation also makes an address resellable. The moment you prove you are a live, responsive reader, your address becomes better inventory, worth promoting to higher-value lists and selling on to other senders. That mechanic is exactly why [unsubscribing from real spam can bring more of it](/blog/more-spam-after-unsubscribing), not less. Your click confirmed the one fact the seller needed. Every open you register does a quieter version of the same thing, which is what the [invisible tracking pixel in most marketing email](/blog/tracking-pixels) exists to measure.

## Why do marketers fight so hard to keep you subscribed?

Because losing you means losing every future dollar that address would earn them. A subscriber is a channel a brand can bill against for years. So the unsubscribe link shrinks to gray footer type, the opt-out becomes a five-step "are you sure" gauntlet, and some senders quietly keep mailing past the point they are allowed to. Under U.S. law they cannot: [a company has ten business days to stop after you opt out](/blog/how-long-after-unsubscribe-can-they-email-you), and consent under GDPR must be as easy to withdraw as it was to give. That friction is a deliberate business decision.

## How do you make your address worth less to them?

You have two moves: give out fewer real addresses, and remove yourself from the lists you are already on. [Masked email aliases](/blog/masked-email-aliases-explained) let you hand every store a different throwaway address that forwards to your real inbox, so a leak or a resale burns the alias instead of you. For the mail already arriving, unsubscribing does more than clear the inbox. It takes you off a real sender's list, and it stops feeding them the opens and clicks that keep your address priced as premium inventory.

## The takeaway

Your email address is cheap in bulk and valuable in use. A scraped address in a breach dump is worth a rounding error, while a confirmed, engaged subscriber is worth $36 or more per dollar to the brand that reaches them. The difference is confirmation, and you control most of it. Hand out fewer real addresses, unsubscribe for real, and stop registering the opens that keep your price high. The less you confirm, the less you are worth to the people you never asked to hear from.
