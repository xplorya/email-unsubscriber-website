Your phone buzzes, and it is not a person. It is Facebook telling you someone you barely know went live, or Instagram reminding you a stranger reacted to a story you posted last week. Both land in your email inbox too, and if you use both apps you are getting two streams of the same noise. The fix is the same in each, because Facebook and Instagram are both Meta apps built on the same settings pattern.

> Facebook and Instagram both put email notifications under Settings, then Notifications, then Email. In Facebook, choose "Only notifications about your account, security and privacy" to mute the rest. In Instagram, open "Emails from Instagram" and turn off the feedback and reminder toggles. Account and security emails stay on in both, by design.

## Why am I getting so many emails from Facebook and Instagram?

Both apps email you every time something happens, and their defaults are turned up high. A like, a comment, a new follower, a tag, a group post, a Marketplace reply, a "you have unseen notifications" nudge: each one can trigger an email unless you tell it not to. Meta owns Facebook and Instagram, so the two apps share the same underlying notification design, which is why the settings look almost identical once you find them.

The volume is not an accident. These emails exist to pull you back into the app, so activity you would never open the app for still generates a message in your inbox. The good news is that Meta groups all of it under one Email section in each app, and the switch that quiets it is a couple of taps away. If your inbox filling up with this kind of automated mail feels like a wider trend, it is; our piece on [why your inbox has more newsletters than it used to](/blog/why-your-inbox-has-more-newsletters) covers what changed across the whole email ecosystem.

## How do I turn off Facebook email notifications?

Set Facebook's email tier to the account-and-security minimum, and every routine activity email stops. Facebook does not offer a hard "off," but its lowest setting silences likes, comments, tags, groups, events, and Marketplace while keeping the emails that protect your login. Here is the desktop path, current as of 2026:

1. **Open Settings.** Click your profile picture in the top right, choose **Settings & Privacy**, then **Settings**.
2. **Go to Notifications.** In the left sidebar, click **Notifications**.
3. **Open the Email section.** Click **Email** to expand it.
4. **Choose the lowest tier.** Facebook shows a few frequency options. Pick **Only notifications about your account, security and privacy**. This is the quietest setting Facebook allows.

On the Facebook mobile app, the route is slightly different: tap the **menu** icon, go to **Settings & Privacy**, then **Settings**, scroll to **Notification Settings**, and open **Email**. Pick the same account-and-security-only option.

Expected outcome: routine activity and promotional emails stop, while password resets, login alerts, and policy notices keep arriving. If you would rather not touch the settings at all, every Facebook email carries an **Unsubscribe** link in its footer, though that only stops the one type of email you clicked it from.

## How do I turn off Instagram email notifications?

Instagram keeps its email controls in a screen called **Emails from Instagram**, and flipping its toggles off ends the activity and reminder mail. Because Instagram is a Meta app, the pattern mirrors Facebook: Settings, then Notifications, then a dedicated Email area. Here is the app path, current as of 2026:

1. **Open your profile.** Tap your profile picture in the bottom-right corner.
2. **Open the menu.** Tap the three-line menu in the top-right corner.
3. **Go to Settings and privacy.** Then scroll to and tap **Notifications**.
4. **Open Emails from Instagram.** Turn off the toggles here. Depending on your app version they are grouped as feedback emails and reminder emails, which cover likes, comments, new followers, and story or reel reminders.

Expected outcome: the promotional and activity emails stop, though Instagram notes changes can take up to 24 hours to fully take effect. Login and security alerts stay on. As with Facebook, exact labels drift between app versions, but the email controls always live inside the **Notifications** menu, kept separate from push notifications so muting one does not touch the other.

## Facebook vs Instagram: where the email setting lives

The destination is the same in both apps, an Email screen under Notifications, but the labels and the last step differ. Here is the side-by-side:

| | Facebook | Instagram |
|---|---|---|
| Owner | Meta | Meta |
| Path | Settings, then Notifications, then Email | Settings and privacy, then Notifications, then Emails from Instagram |
| Control type | A frequency tier you pick | Individual on/off toggles |
| Quietest option | Only notifications about your account, security and privacy | All feedback and reminder toggles off |
| Can't be turned off | Account, security, privacy emails | Login and security alerts |
| Footer unsubscribe | Yes, per email type | Yes, per email type |

Bottom line: in Facebook you drop to the lowest tier, in Instagram you switch off each toggle. Neither gives you a total blackout, because both hold back a small set of security emails on purpose.

## What is the difference between the in-app setting and the unsubscribe link?

The in-app setting is the source-level off switch; the footer unsubscribe link only stops one kind of email. When you change the Email setting inside Facebook or Instagram, you are telling Meta to stop generating whole categories of mail. When you click **Unsubscribe** at the bottom of a single email, you usually opt out of just that one stream, say friend suggestions, and the likes, comments, and reminders keep coming from their own separate toggles.

Use the footer link when one specific recurring email annoys you and you want it gone in five seconds. Use the in-app setting when you want the whole flood to stop at once. For senders that are not Meta and refuse to honor either, blocking is the blunt fallback, and our guide to [blocking an email sender in Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail](/blog/how-to-block-email-sender) walks that route.

## Which Facebook and Instagram emails can't you turn off?

Account, security, and privacy emails stay on in both apps, and you cannot switch them off. That covers password resets, login-from-a-new-device alerts, two-factor codes, and changes to Meta's terms or policies. Facebook's lowest email tier is literally named for this category, and Instagram keeps its login and security alerts on regardless of your other toggles.

This floor is deliberate, and it is worth keeping. Those emails are how you find out if someone else tries to get into your account. The senders behind them, addresses like `security@facebookmail.com`, are the ones you should never unsubscribe from or block, because doing so cuts off your early warning system. Everything else, the engagement bait and the promotions, is fair game to silence.

## Why do the emails keep coming after I turned them off?

Give it up to 24 hours, and check whether you missed a toggle. Instagram says changes apply immediately but some emails can take a full day to stop, and Facebook behaves the same way. A message or two right after you change the setting is normal, not a failure.

If ordinary activity or promotional emails are still landing after a day, one of two things is usually going on. You muted one category but left another switched on, so revisit the Email screen and confirm each toggle saved. Or the email that keeps arriving is an account, security, or privacy notice, which neither app lets you disable. Sort which bucket the stubborn email falls into, and the fix follows: another toggle to flip, or an email you are meant to keep.

Facebook and Instagram are the same problem twice, and the same fix twice. Both bury their email controls under Settings, then Notifications, then an Email screen.
