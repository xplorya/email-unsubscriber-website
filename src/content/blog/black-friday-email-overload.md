You open Gmail the Monday before Thanksgiving and the unread count has doubled overnight. Every store you ever bought a phone case from wants you to know about its early-access doorbuster, and three of them email twice a day until December. The flood is not your imagination, and it is not random.

> Black Friday email overload is real and measurable. Brands sent 59.9 billion messages from Black Friday to Cyber Monday 2025, up 19 percent year over year, according to Braze. Senders front-load these windows because most holiday spending happens in a few days, so they crowd your inbox to win the click before a competitor does.

## How much does email volume actually jump around Black Friday?

The jump is counted in billions of extra messages, not a vague "more." The major sending platforms publish their weekend totals, and the totals climb every year.

According to [Braze's BFCM 2025 report](https://www.braze.com/resources/articles/black-friday-cyber-monday-trends-2025) (December 2025), brands sent 59.9 billion messages across its platform from Black Friday to Cyber Monday, a 19 percent increase over 2024, with email volume alone up 32 percent year over year. Cyber Monday carried 17.0 billion of those messages, a 28 percent jump and the first time in Braze's history that Cyber Monday beat Black Friday.

Two other platforms report the same shape. Sinch's Mailjet and Mailgun delivered 20.4 billion emails during the week of Black Friday, a 32 percent year-over-year surge in November email volume, per [Sinch's BFCM analysis](https://www.mailjet.com/blog/email-best-practices/bfcm-stats-2025/) (December 2025). The messaging platform Bird processed 19.9 billion emails across the same weekend, nearly 20 percent more than 2024, and peaked at 347,678 messages per second on Black Friday, according to [Bird's by-the-numbers recap](https://bird.com/en-us/blog/bird-s-black-friday-by-the-numbers) (December 2025). Your single inbox sits at the receiving end of all of it.

## Why do brands front-load Black Friday and Cyber Monday?

Brands crowd the window because the spending crowds the window. When a huge share of the quarter's sales concentrates into a few days, a marketing team cannot send one polite email and hope.

Holiday shopping concentrates heavily into Cyber Week, and every retailer knows a subscriber's attention and wallet both run out fast that week. So the calculus flips from "do not annoy them" to "reach them before the competitor does." A brand that sends once risks getting buried under a rival that sends three times. That arms race is why the same store mails you a teaser on Tuesday, an early-access note on Wednesday, the real sale on Friday, a "last chance" on Saturday, and a Cyber Monday extension on Monday.

It is the seasonal exception to a year-round trend. Many brands now [email you less the rest of the year](/blog/why-brands-email-you-less), because mailbox providers punish over-sending, but the Black Friday window is where even the disciplined senders open the taps.

## Does Prime Day cause the same email flood?

Yes. Amazon Prime Day is the summer rehearsal for the same pattern, and it is no longer a small event. According to [Adobe Analytics](https://business.adobe.com/blog/prime-day-event-drove-24-billion-in-online-spend-across-us-retailers) (July 2025), U.S. shoppers spent 24.1 billion dollars online during the four-day Prime Day event in July 2025, up 30.3 percent year over year and more than two Black Fridays' worth of online spend.

The email flood around Prime Day is not only Amazon's doing. Competing retailers run "anti-Prime-Day" and "summer sale" campaigns timed to the same days, because the shopping intent is already switched on. Your inbox gets the July version of the teaser-to-last-chance sequence from stores that have nothing to do with Amazon. Any concentrated retail moment, from Prime Day to back-to-school to Memorial Day, produces a smaller copy of the Black Friday spike.

## Why do so many of these emails land in Promotions or spam?

They land there because mailbox providers get stricter exactly when senders get louder. Gmail routes most bulk retail promotions to the Promotions tab by design, and the holiday surge pushes even more of them out of the primary inbox.

There is a deliverability reason underneath it. According to [Google's email sender guidelines](https://support.google.com/mail/answer/81126), a bulk sender must keep its spam-complaint rate below 0.1 percent and never let it reach 0.3 percent, or Gmail starts filtering its mail. When a brand triples its volume in a week and blasts subscribers who have not opened anything in months, complaints climb and reputation drops, so more of that sender's mail lands in spam, and not only for you. The irony of the arms race is that the brands sending the most are the ones most likely to get filtered. For how volume, spam, and open rates move together the rest of the year, our [email overload statistics roundup](/blog/email-overload-statistics) collects the sourced numbers.

## How do you survive the seasonal email flood?

You survive it by doing the cleanup before the wave, not during it. Four moves, in order of impact.

1. **Run a pre-season unsubscribe sweep.** In late October, or early June for Prime Day, go through your subscription senders and opt out of the stores you no longer buy from. Clearing them before the campaigns start means the blast never reaches you. Our guide to [mass unsubscribing in Gmail](/blog/mass-unsubscribe-gmail) covers the built-in and faster routes.
2. **Filter the survivors into a folder.** For senders you want to keep but not see every hour, create a filter that labels their mail and skips the inbox. You get the deal when you go looking for it, without the 6 a.m. interruption.
3. **Mute the loudest threads.** When one brand runs a five-email "last chance" sequence, muting or archiving the thread stops the follow-ups from resurfacing, without unsubscribing outright.
4. **Route shopping to a separate address.** Give stores a dedicated email at checkout so new sign-ups never touch your primary inbox. The promo flood lands somewhere you can ignore.

Do the first move and the other three shrink. A sweep before the season starts is the difference between an inbox you skim and one you dread.

## Will unsubscribing before Black Friday actually stick?

Yes, for legitimate senders, though you should expect some refill. A real unsubscribe removes you from the list, and under Gmail and Yahoo's bulk-sender rules the one-click opt-out has to be honored quickly. A store that keeps mailing you weeks after a clean unsubscribe is in violation, not just annoying.

The catch is that unsubscribing is a one-time subtraction while subscribing is continuous. Every Black Friday purchase, coupon grab, and account sign-up quietly adds you to a new list, so the inbox that took an afternoon to clear starts refilling by spring. That treadmill is why [subscription fatigue](/blog/email-subscription-fatigue) sets in within about 90 days, and why the sweep works best as a seasonal habit rather than a one-off. Clear it before Black Friday, clear it again before Prime Day, and each pass gets shorter.

## What the seasonal spike means for your inbox

The seasonal spike is the one time of year even the disciplined senders blast you, so the mail you opted into and the mail you forgot about all arrive at once. Ignoring it does nothing, and marking it as spam punishes senders you might still want. The move that works is the one it always is: unsubscribe from what you no longer want, before the wave rather than during it.
