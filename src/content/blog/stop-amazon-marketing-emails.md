Amazon emails you more than almost anyone. Daily deals, Kindle picks, "customers who bought this also bought," Prime Video reminders, and a fresh recommendation every time you glance at a product page. You clicked unsubscribe on one, and three others kept arriving, because Amazon runs your mail across a dozen separate lists rather than one.

> On a desktop browser, sign in and open Accounts & Lists, then Your Account, then Communication preferences. Under the email settings, select "Do not send me any marketing email for now" and Save. That stops the promotional mail. Order and shipping confirmations are transactional and keep coming. Service newsletters like Kindle and Audible are separate, under Email subscriptions.

## Why does Amazon keep emailing after I unsubscribe?

Amazon splits your mail across many lists, so leaving one list does nothing to the others. Deals sit on one program, recommendations on another, and Kindle, Audible, Prime Video, and Amazon Music each run their own. Click the Unsubscribe link at the foot of a Kindle Daily Deals email and you leave that single list. The recommendation engine, the Prime Video nudges, and the storewide promotions never got the message.

This is the core of the problem, and it is why a one-off unsubscribe feels like it fails. You did opt out. You just opted out of one channel out of many. To actually go quiet, you set the account-level marketing switch that covers the bulk of it, then clear the service newsletters that live in a second panel. Two settings, not one, and both sit behind a menu most people never open.

There is also a line Amazon draws that no setting crosses. Marketing email is optional and you can leave it. Order confirmations, shipping updates, and delivery notices are transactional, tied to a purchase you made, and Amazon sends them regardless. Knowing which bucket a message falls into tells you whether a setting exists for it at all.

## How do I turn off Amazon marketing emails?

You turn off Amazon's main promotional mail from the Communications Preference Center, on a desktop browser. As of 2026, the path runs through your account menu, and Amazon occasionally relabels these screens, so match the intent if a word has shifted.

1. **Sign in on a computer.** Open amazon.com in a normal browser. The mobile app and mobile site hide several of these controls, so a desktop browser is the reliable route.
2. **Open your account.** Click **Accounts & Lists** in the top-right corner, then **Your Account**.
3. **Find Communication preferences.** Scroll to the **Communication and content** section and click **Communication preferences**.
4. **Stop the marketing email.** Under the email settings, select **"Do not send me any marketing email for now,"** then click **Save changes**. Amazon's own [unsubscribe help page](https://www.amazon.com/gp/help/customer/display.html?nodeId=GJAW9SFGM8XTS89H) walks the same route.
5. **Cut the direct mail too, if you want.** In the same center, expand the **Mail** category and uncheck the option to get occasional marketing about products, deals, and recommendations in the post, then Save.

Expected outcome: Amazon's storewide promotional emails stop landing, usually within a few days. This one switch is the heaviest lever, and for most people it removes the majority of the flood in a single click.

## How do I stop Amazon newsletters like Kindle and Audible?

You switch those off under Email subscriptions, a separate list from the marketing toggle above. Amazon treats Kindle, Audible, Prime Video, Amazon Music, and its other services as their own email programs, so the main opt-out does not touch them.

From the same **Communication and content** section, click **Email subscriptions** (also reachable at amazon.com/gp/subscriptions). Amazon shows every newsletter, product update, and alert you are signed up for. Toggle off the ones you never read: Kindle Daily Deals, Kindle Monthly Editors' Picks, Audible sale alerts, and the rest, one by one. Amazon's [subscription-preferences page](https://www.amazon.com/gp/help/customer/display.html?nodeId=GXSTUM6E6RZDAQRY) covers this second panel and notes changes can take up to five business days to register.

The tedium here is real. There is no master switch for the service newsletters, so you clear them individually, and a service you enroll in later adds a fresh line. This is the same seasonal churn that fills every inbox around [Black Friday and Prime Day](/blog/black-friday-email-overload), when Amazon and every store timing a sale to the same days open the taps at once.

## Can I stop order and shipping confirmation emails?

No. Amazon offers no setting to turn off order confirmations, shipping updates, or delivery notices, because those are transactional messages tied to a purchase. They are not marketing, so no unsubscribe option exists for them anywhere in your account.

If they clutter your inbox, the fix lives in your own mail app rather than in Amazon's settings. Create a filter that catches mail from Amazon's order addresses and routes it to a label or the archive, so it skips your inbox while staying searchable when a package goes missing. Our guide to [blocking and filtering a sender](/blog/how-to-block-email-sender) walks through building that rule in Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail. Filter these; do not block them, or you lose the delivery update you might need.

## What is the difference between communication preferences and advertising preferences?

Communication preferences control the email in your inbox. Advertising preferences control the ads on your screen. They are two separate systems, and people opening the wrong one is a common reason the emails never stop.

Amazon's [Advertising Preferences page](https://www.amazon.com/adprefs) governs interest-based ads: the banners Amazon shows you on its site, across the web, and on affiliate sites, based on your browsing and purchase history. Selecting "Do not show me interest-based ads" there makes those ads generic. It does nothing to your email. If your goal is fewer messages, the ad settings are the wrong lever entirely, and the communication preferences above are the one that matters.

| Lever | Where it lives | What it changes |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing email opt-out | Communication preferences | Storewide promotional and recommendation emails |
| Email subscriptions | Communication preferences, Email subscriptions | Per-service newsletters (Kindle, Audible, Prime Video) |
| Advertising preferences | amazon.com/adprefs | Interest-based ads on and off Amazon, not email |
| Order and shipping mail | No setting | Transactional, cannot be turned off |

The takeaway from the table: two of these four levers touch your email, one touches ads, and one cannot be changed at all. Aim at the first two.

## How long until Amazon emails actually stop?

A few days, in most cases. Amazon says the change takes some time to register, and its subscription-preferences page lists up to five business days for email settings to take effect. Give it a week before you decide the switch failed.

There is a legal backstop too. Under the US CAN-SPAM Act, a sender has ten business days to honor an opt-out request, per the [FTC's compliance guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business). If Amazon promotional mail is still arriving two or three weeks after you saved the setting, the change likely did not stick. Sign in on a desktop browser and confirm the marketing toggle is still off, since a later click somewhere in your account can quietly flip it back.

## What about the unsubscribe link at the bottom of the email?

That link works, but it only leaves the one list. Every Amazon marketing email carries an **Unsubscribe** link in its footer, and clicking it removes you from that specific program and drops you into your communication preferences. Because Amazon mails you from many separate lists, the next campaign from a different list still shows up the next morning.

The link has one quiet advantage worth knowing. When your mail app exposes its own unsubscribe control, built from the message's List-Unsubscribe header, you can opt out [without opening the email at all](/blog/unsubscribe-without-opening-email), so no tracking pixel fires and nothing confirms your address is active. That is the cleaner move for a single sender.

For years of buildup across Amazon's many lists plus every other store in your inbox, clearing them one footer link at a time is slow. The scan runs on your device, so we never read, analyze, or monetize your email content, the access is read-only, and the session expires in about an hour with no lingering token. Its "still emailing" filter also flags the senders that keep mailing after you left, so you know exactly which ones ignored the request.

## The takeaway

Stopping Amazon marketing email is a two-setting job, both behind Communication preferences on a desktop browser. Set "Do not send me any marketing email for now" to kill the storewide promotions, then open Email subscriptions and switch off the service newsletters one by one. Order and shipping confirmations are transactional and stay, so filter those in your own mail app if they crowd you. Leave the advertising preferences alone unless you also want fewer targeted ads, because that page never touches your inbox. Give the change up to about a week, and if the promos outlast the CAN-SPAM window, re-check that the toggle saved.
