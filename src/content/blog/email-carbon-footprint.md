You saw the post telling you to delete your old emails to save the planet, and now you are staring at twelve thousand archived messages wondering whether an afternoon of trashing them is worth it. It is a fair question. The answer is not the one the viral posts give you.

> Deleting stored emails does almost nothing for the climate. An archived message sits on a data center that runs 24/7 whether it exists or not, so deleting it saves close to zero. Email's real footprint lives in the devices you use and in the messages senders keep transmitting. Stopping unwanted sends beats emptying your archive.

The carbon cost of email is real but small, and most of the popular advice about it points at the wrong lever. This piece walks through what a single email actually costs, why deleting old mail barely registers, where the footprint really sits, and the one move that does more than the rest. Every figure carries a named source and a date.

## How much CO2 does one email actually produce?

Nobody can give you a precise number, because the honest answer is a wide range. The most-cited estimates come from Mike Berners-Lee, a Lancaster University researcher whose book [How Bad Are Bananas? The Carbon Footprint of Everything](https://howbadarebananas.com/) put figures on hundreds of everyday things. In the 2020 revised edition he pegs a single email somewhere between 0.03 grams and 26 grams of CO2 equivalent, depending mostly on attachments and how long a human spends at the keyboard.

| Type of email | Estimated carbon | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Spam caught by a filter | 0.03 g CO2e | Berners-Lee, 2020 |
| Short email from a phone | 0.2 g CO2e | Berners-Lee, 2020 |
| Short email from a laptop | 0.3 g CO2e | Berners-Lee, 2020 |
| Long email, 10 min to write, 3 to read | 17 g CO2e | Berners-Lee, 2020 |
| Email blast to 100 people, one reads it | 26 g CO2e | Berners-Lee, 2020 |

Notice how much the writing time and the attachment matter. A quick note tapped out on your phone costs a fraction of a gram. A long message with a photo, multiplied across a big recipient list, costs a hundred times more. The 2010 first edition of the same book quoted higher numbers still, roughly 4 grams for a standard email and 50 grams for one with a large attachment, and that older figure is where most of the scary headlines got their math. Treat all of these as rough estimates that vary with your device, your grid, and your habits. No single number is gospel.

## Does deleting old emails reduce your carbon footprint?

Barely, and if you do it by hand you can come out behind. A stored email lives on a server that a data center keeps powered around the clock, whether your message is there or not. The building draws a near-constant base load, so pulling one email out of it saves almost nothing. Deleting mail does not spin down a machine.

The arithmetic gets worse when you count your own time. Researchers Luciano Rodrigues Viana, Jean-Francois Boucher, and Mohamed Cheriet, writing in [The Conversation](https://theconversation.com/can-sending-fewer-emails-or-emptying-your-inbox-really-help-fight-climate-change-193822) in December 2022, worked out that spending 30 minutes on a laptop deleting 1,000 emails emits roughly 28 grams of CO2 equivalent on a high-carbon grid, against the roughly 5 grams that deletion actually saves. Sitting there tapping delete for half an hour burns more power than the cleanup recovers. If you want a smaller footprint, the last thing to do is spend a long screen session culling your archive.

## Where does email's carbon footprint actually come from?

Most of it comes from the hardware in your hands, not the mail in the cloud. Building a phone or a laptop is carbon-intensive, and running one draws power the whole time you read and write. Transmitting the message across the network adds a little on top. The stored copy sitting in a data center is the cheapest part of the whole chain.

Email is also a rounding error inside internet traffic as a whole. The same Conversation analysis notes that email is about 1 percent of internet traffic, while video streaming is around 82 percent. So email is a small slice of a small slice of your personal emissions. Meanwhile roughly half of all email sent is spam, a scale we cover in our [roundup of email overload statistics](/blog/email-overload-statistics), which tells you where the volume genuinely piles up. The clutter problem in your inbox is real. The climate problem attached to it is minor.

## Where did "delete emails to save the planet" come from?

The idea traces to one widely covered study. In November 2019 the energy company OVO Energy published research titled [Think Before You Thank](https://company.ovo.com/think-before-you-thank-if-every-brit-sent-one-less-thank-you-email-a-day-we-would-save-16433-tonnes-of-carbon-a-year-the-same-as-81152-flights-to-madrid/), which claimed that if every adult in Britain sent one fewer email a day, the UK would save 16,433 tonnes of carbon a year, the same as 81,152 flights to Madrid. The survey found Brits send over 64 million unnecessary emails a day, many of them one-line "thank you" replies. Berners-Lee advised on the project.

The number that drove those headlines does not hold up. The study assumed one unnecessary email emits about 1 gram of CO2. George Kamiya, then an energy analyst at the [International Energy Agency](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/the-carbon-footprint-of-streaming-video-fact-checking-the-headlines), wrote in December 2020 that this figure came from back-of-the-envelope calculations from a decade earlier, that emissions from email today are much lower, and that such headlines "vastly overestimate the potential emission reductions from avoided emails." The takeaway that skipping a thank-you note saves the planet was built on a stale estimate.

## So is deleting emails ever worth it?

Yes, but for reasons that have nothing to do with carbon. Clearing out an archive helps you find things faster, frees up storage if you are near a quota, and gives you a calmer inbox to work in. Those are good reasons to tidy up. Saving the climate is not one of them, and any tool that promises a meaningful carbon reduction from bulk-deleting your mail is selling you a feeling.

So delete freely when it helps you, and do not feel guilty when you leave a decade of receipts sitting in a folder. The stored copy is the cheapest thing in the entire email system. Your attention is worth more than the fraction of a gram you would recover.

## Is unsubscribing better for the planet than deleting?

A little, and it beats deleting on the merits. Deleting removes a message that already cost its carbon to send and now costs almost nothing to store. Unsubscribing stops the next message before a sender ever generates and transmits it, and transmission is where email's small footprint actually sits. Stopping a send avoids a cost. Deleting a stored copy recovers a cost that was already near zero.

Be honest about the size of it. For one person, unsubscribing from a promo list is a trivial climate action, and anyone who tells you otherwise is greenwashing. The reason to do it is that your inbox gets quieter, and the tiny environmental effect rides along for free. That effect grows a bit when you remember a marketing list mails the same message to hundreds of thousands of people, so opting out removes your slice of a send that repeats at scale. [Email Unsubscriber](https://app.email-unsubscriber.com) scans your Gmail or Outlook in your browser, lists every subscription sender, and lets you opt out in a few clicks, without your email content ever reaching a server.

## What actually shrinks your digital carbon footprint?

Keeping your devices longer does more than any inbox habit. Because manufacturing hardware dominates the footprint, the researchers in The Conversation recommend buying fewer gadgets, holding on to the ones you have, and choosing lower-power equipment. A phone kept for four years instead of two does far more good than any amount of email housekeeping. Streaming less high-definition video helps too, since video is the giant that email is not.

Set the inbox advice in that context. Trimming email is fine, but it belongs near the bottom of the list of things that matter, well below the devices you buy and the video you stream. The honest version of the story is quieter than the viral one. Your inbox is not a climate emergency, and treating it like one wastes effort that would count for more somewhere else.

## The bottom line

Email carbon is real, small, and mostly out of your hands. A single message costs a fraction of a gram to a couple dozen grams, the stored copy costs almost nothing, and deleting old mail by hand can burn more power than it saves. The "delete to save the planet" advice rests on a decade-old estimate that experts have walked back.

If you want to act on the email angle, stopping unwanted sends beats emptying your archive, and it comes with the bigger prize of an inbox you actually want to open. Do it for the quiet, and let the rounding-error carbon savings be a bonus you never have to think about again.
