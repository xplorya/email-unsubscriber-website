You open your inbox after a long weekend and find four hundred unread messages, most of them newsletters and promos you do not remember signing up for. It feels like the volume keeps climbing every year. It does, and the research backs up the feeling with hard numbers.

> Email overload is measurable. Statista put global volume near 376 billion messages a day in 2025, and Kaspersky found 44.99% of it was spam. McKinsey clocked knowledge workers at 28% of the workweek on email, and Optimove reported that 70% of consumers unsubscribed from at least three brands in a single quarter.

This is a roundup of the email-overload numbers worth citing in 2026, grouped by what they measure: total volume, per-person load, time cost, spam, subscription fatigue, and what people actually do about it. Every figure carries a named source and a year. Where a widely quoted number gets its scope wrong, the note explains why.

## How many emails are sent every day?

The world moves hundreds of billions of emails a day, and the count keeps rising by roughly four percent a year. This is the top-line volume figure most articles reach for, and it is worth stating with its source attached.

- According to Statista's email-traffic data, which draws on Radicati Group estimates, the world sent and received roughly **376 billion emails a day in 2025**, a total projected to pass **400 billion a day by 2027**.
- According to the [Radicati Group's Email Statistics Report, 2024-2028](https://www.einpresswire.com/article/751597875/the-radicati-group-releases-email-statistics-report-2024-2028) (published December 2024), there were **more than 4.4 billion email users worldwide in 2024**, a base the firm expects to grow past **4.9 billion by 2028**.

Those two figures set the scale. More people are sending more mail every year, and consumer inboxes absorb a growing share of it as marketers, apps, and services all default to email for outreach.

## How many emails does one person get in a day?

The honest answer is that it depends on the mailbox, and the most-quoted number measures the wrong one. You will see "the average person gets over a hundred emails a day" repeated everywhere. It comes from a business-mailbox study, not a personal inbox.

According to the [Radicati Group's Email Statistics Report, 2024-2028](https://www.einpresswire.com/article/751597875/the-radicati-group-releases-email-statistics-report-2024-2028), a typical **business user sends and receives about 126 emails a day**. That figure counts a full corporate mailbox: reply-all threads, calendar invites, internal CCs, ticket notifications, and automated alerts. It is a real number, but it describes a work account under load, not the Gmail or Outlook account where your newsletters pile up.

A personal inbox usually carries fewer genuine messages and a heavier share of marketing and automated mail. So when a stat claims you personally receive 120-plus emails a day, check whether it is quoting business-user data. Most of the time it is. The overload in a personal inbox is less about raw count and more about signal-to-noise: the mail that matters buried under the mail that does not.

## How much time does email actually cost?

Email eats a large, measurable slice of the workday for anyone who works at a desk. This is the productivity angle, and the anchor number is old but still the one researchers cite.

According to the McKinsey Global Institute's report [The Social Economy](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-social-economy), published in July 2012, interaction workers spend about **28 percent of the workweek, roughly 13 hours, reading, writing, and answering email**. McKinsey defines interaction workers as managers, professionals, and salespeople whose jobs run on communication.

Two caveats keep this stat honest. It is from 2012, so treat it as the durable benchmark it has become rather than a fresh reading. And it measures time on the job, not the minutes you lose triaging promos in your personal inbox on a Sunday. Even so, it captures the core point: email is not a quick task you check between other work. For a lot of people it is a meaningful part of the work itself.

## How much of your inbox is spam?

Close to half of all email traffic is spam, and a slice of it is actively hostile. This is one of the best-sourced numbers in the whole category, updated yearly by a security vendor with global telemetry.

- According to [Kaspersky's Spam and Phishing Report for 2025](https://securelist.com/spam-and-phishing-report-2025/118785/), **44.99 percent of all email sent worldwide in 2025 was spam**. Almost every second message.
- The same report says Kaspersky blocked **144,722,674 malicious email attachments** and stopped **554,002,207 attempts to follow phishing links** over the year.

Spam is the loudest layer of overload, but it is not the only one. A lot of what buries a personal inbox is technically legitimate: marketing you once opted into, receipts, shipping updates, and newsletters you forgot about. Much of that legitimate mail also carries hidden [tracking pixels](/blog/tracking-pixels) that report back when and where you open it, which is a separate privacy cost on top of the clutter.

## How burned out are people on email subscriptions?

Subscription fatigue is widespread and it changes behavior. People are not just annoyed by the volume; they are actively cutting senders and, in some cases, switching brands over it.

- According to [Optimove's 2025 Consumer Marketing Fatigue Report](https://www.optimove.com/resources/reports/2025-optimove-insights-consumer-marketing-fatigue-full-report), based on a survey of 329 U.S. consumers, **70 percent unsubscribed from at least three brands in the previous three months** because of message volume, and **37 percent named email the most annoying channel** for excessive communication.
- The same report found **57 percent of consumers switched to a competitor** after feeling overwhelmed by a brand's marketing.
- According to a [GetApp advertising-preferences survey reported in October 2024](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20241007772448/en/40-of-U.S.-Consumers-Unsubscribe-From-Marketing-Emails-WeeklyStrategies-for-Businesses-to-Re-Engage), **40 percent of U.S. consumers unsubscribe from brand emails or texts at least once a week**, well above the 25 percent global average, and **56 percent unsubscribe after four or more messages** from one company within 30 days.

The pattern is clear. Overload has a tipping point, people know when they have hit it, and the response is to leave. If your inbox is at that point, the fix is to clear the backlog in one sitting rather than picking off senders one email at a time. ## What do people do with unwanted email?

Most people ignore unwanted mail, and the second-most-common move quietly hurts senders more than unsubscribing does. This is the behavioral data, and it explains a lot about why inboxes stay cluttered.

According to a 2025 survey of more than a thousand U.S. consumers by [Orbit Media Studios](https://www.orbitmedia.com/blog/spam-statistics/), run with QuestionPro, here is what people do when an unwanted email lands:

- **50 percent ignore it.**
- **30 percent report it as spam.**
- **10 percent unsubscribe.**
- **9 percent report and block the sender.**

Reporting as spam is three times more common than unsubscribing. That matters, because ignoring mail does nothing to stop it, and marking a legitimate sender as spam punishes their reputation without cleaning your inbox. Unsubscribing is the move that actually removes you from the list, yet it is the one people reach for least, often because they are not sure the link is safe to click. It usually is, and there is a [30-second way to tell](/blog/is-it-safe-to-click-unsubscribe) the real links from the traps. Which of the two to reach for in any given case comes down to a short call: [unsubscribe or mark as spam](/blog/unsubscribe-vs-mark-as-spam).

## Are marketing emails still getting opened?

Reported open rates look healthy, but the headline number is inflated by a privacy feature, and unsubscribe rates are climbing fast. This is the sender-side data, and it needs a caveat to read correctly.

According to [MailerLite's 2025 email-marketing benchmarks](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks), drawn from 3.6 million campaigns across more than 181,000 accounts between December 2024 and November 2025:

- The average **open rate was 43.46 percent**, up from 42.35 percent in 2024.
- The average **unsubscribe rate was 0.22 percent**, more than double 2024's 0.08 percent.
- The average **click rate was 2.09 percent**, and the click-to-open rate was 6.81 percent.

The open-rate figure comes with an asterisk. Apple Mail Privacy Protection automatically marks messages as opened for Apple Mail users whether or not they open them, which inflates open rates across the whole industry. Real engagement runs lower than 43 percent. The more telling trend is the unsubscribe rate doubling in a year, which lines up with Gmail and Yahoo making one-click unsubscribe easier and with the fatigue numbers above. People are opting out in larger numbers than before.

## What these numbers mean for your inbox

Read together, the statistics tell one story. Global volume keeps rising, close to half of it is spam, subscription fatigue is mainstream, and the most common response, ignoring the mail, does nothing to fix it. The gap between how people feel about their inbox and what they do about it is the whole problem.

The durable fix is not another filter or a mass delete, both of which hide mail without stopping it.
