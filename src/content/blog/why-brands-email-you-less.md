You noticed it without naming it. A couple of brands you actually like went quiet this year, dropping from three emails a week to one, while the sketchy senders you never signed up for still land in your inbox every morning.

> Many brands cut their email volume in 2025 and 2026 because mailbox providers now reward engagement and punish complaints. Gmail keeps a sender's spam-complaint rate under a strict limit, so a low-relevance blast that triggers unsubscribes damages delivery to everyone on the list. Sending less, and more relevant, is the safer play. The shift is real but uneven.

## Why are brands sending fewer emails now?

Brands are sending fewer emails because the math that rewarded volume stopped paying out. For years a marketer could blast an entire list and treat a 10 percent open rate as a win. In 2026 the industry consensus flipped, and the trade reports say so plainly.

According to Mailjet's [Email Marketing Trends 2026 report](https://www.mailjet.com/blog/email-best-practices/email-marketing-trends-2026/) (December 2025), the brands that win now "build programs that send fewer but far more impactful messages informed by behavior, lifecycle stage, and customer intent." The report's blunt version comes from Jonathan Campbell, VP of Product Messaging at Sinch, who argues marketers "must move away from the traditional marketing math of send a million messages and hope that 10 percent get opened." PPR Strategies, [writing in January 2026](https://pprstrategies.com/what-effective-email-marketing-looks-like-in-2026/), reaches the same conclusion: "Success comes from precision, not volume," and the strongest programs now send "fewer newsletters, more behavior-driven messages tied to real signals."

The phrase making the rounds among email teams is that relevance is the new deliverability. A brand that respects your attention earns a spot in your inbox. A brand that floods you gets filtered.

## What changed for the brands that got quieter?

What changed is that mailbox providers turned high volume into a delivery risk. Gmail and Yahoo rewrote the rules for bulk senders, in force since February 2024, and the requirements target complaints and engagement rather than raw send count.

The number that scares email teams is the spam-complaint rate. According to Google's [Email sender guidelines](https://support.google.com/mail/answer/81126), a bulk sender must keep the spam rate reported in Postmaster Tools below 0.1 percent and should never let it reach 0.3 percent or higher. Cross 0.3 percent and Google makes the sender ineligible for delivery mitigation until the rate stays under the line for seven straight days. At 0.1 percent, a single complaint per thousand emails is enough to matter, so a brand mailing a million addresses cannot afford a blast that annoys even a small fraction of them.

The same rules require every marketing message to carry a one-click unsubscribe under RFC 8058, and Google expects senders to process those opt-outs within 48 hours. Opting out got easier by mandate. When leaving a list takes one tap, a brand that over-sends watches its audience walk out the door, and each departure is a signal the provider records.

## What is engagement-based inbox placement?

Engagement-based inbox placement is mailbox providers deciding where your mail lands, inbox or spam, based on how you and people like you have treated a sender before. Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo watch whether recipients open, reply, delete without reading, or mark as spam, and they route future mail accordingly.

It matters because it makes your behavior the sender's report card. When you ignore a brand's mail for months, the provider reads that as a weak signal and starts pushing the sender toward the Promotions tab or the spam folder, and not only for you but across the sender's whole reputation. Mailjet's 2026 report describes the result as intelligent inboxes that filter on demonstrable value rather than message quantity. A brand cannot volume its way past a filter that is measuring whether anyone wants the mail.

## Why does one spammy blast hurt a brand's other emails?

One careless send hurts everything a brand mails afterward because deliverability is scored at the sender level, not the message level. A batch that provokes complaints and unsubscribes drags down the reputation attached to the sending domain, and the provider applies that reputation to the next campaign too.

The unsubscribe signal is climbing, which tells you people are using the exits. According to [MailerLite's 2025 benchmarks](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks), drawn from 3.6 million campaigns, the average unsubscribe rate reached 0.22 percent, more than double the 0.08 percent of a year earlier. Every one of those opt-outs is a recipient telling the provider the mail was not wanted. Consumers reinforce it with their feet. [Optimove's 2025 Consumer Marketing Fatigue Report](https://www.optimove.com/resources/reports/2025-optimove-insights-consumer-marketing-fatigue-full-report) found that 57 percent of consumers switched to a competitor after feeling overwhelmed by a brand's marketing. A blast that lifts one campaign's revenue can cost a brand its inbox access and some of its customers, which is why the careful teams stopped blasting.

## Why did a brand you ignored suddenly go silent?

A brand went silent on you because it deliberately stopped mailing a subscriber who never engages, a practice email teams call sunsetting. If you have not opened a sender's mail in six months, keeping you on the list is a liability under engagement-based placement, so the smart move is to drop you before your silence drags the sender's reputation down.

This is the counterintuitive part of the shift. The brands doing it right would rather lose a dormant address than keep mailing it, because a list of people who actually open is worth more to a provider than a big list full of ghosts. PPR Strategies frames the 2026 playbook as replacing habitual sends with intentional ones aimed at people who show real signals. If a store you bought from once in 2023 quietly disappeared from your inbox, you were probably sunset. The brand read your silence correctly and moved on.

## So why does your inbox still feel full?

Your inbox still feels full because the restraint trend is real but deeply uneven, and the senders ignoring it are the loudest ones you have. Frequency discipline is a choice, and plenty of brands have not made it.

The over-senders are still out there in force. According to a [GetApp advertising-preferences survey reported in October 2024](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20241007772448/en/40-of-U.S.-Consumers-Unsubscribe-From-Marketing-Emails-WeeklyStrategies-for-Businesses-to-Re-Engage), 56 percent of U.S. consumers unsubscribe after four or more messages from one company within 30 days, which means a lot of companies are still mailing four-plus times a month and losing people for it. Two other forces push volume up. Generative AI has made producing an email nearly free, so the brands chasing volume can blast more of it faster, a shift we cover in our look at whether [AI is writing the emails you get](/blog/is-ai-writing-your-emails). The newsletter format also keeps multiplying independent of any brand's restraint, which our piece on [why your inbox has more newsletters than ever](/blog/why-your-inbox-has-more-newsletters) unpacks. The careful brands got quieter. The careless ones and the machines got louder, and the net effect on your inbox leans full.

## What the volume shift means for your inbox

For you, the volume shift sorts your senders into two piles. The brands that quieted down and started sending mail you occasionally want are the ones worth keeping, because they are reading the same signals you send and adjusting to them. The senders still blasting you four times a week are the ones the new rules are trying to push out, and you can finish the job by unsubscribing.

That second pile is where a cleanup pays off. Ignoring the mail does nothing, since engagement-based placement only nudges a persistent over-sender toward the Promotions tab, not out of your life. A clean unsubscribe removes you from the list, and under the same bulk-sender rules the one-click opt-out has to be honored fast.
